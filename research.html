Research

Our work spans four interconnected programmes tackling distinct but related dimensions of metabolic disease: white adipose tissue expandability, lipotoxicity, brown fat thermogenesis, and the immunometabolic function of adipose macrophages.

Adipose Biology White fat · Energy storage · Obesity

White Adipose Tissue Expandability

White adipose tissue (WAT) acts as the primary buffer for surplus dietary energy. We investigate the molecular determinants of WAT expandability — the genes, signalling pathways, and epigenetic regulators that allow adipocytes to safely hypertrophy and undergo hyperplasia — and the consequences when this capacity is exceeded, leading to ectopic lipid deposition in liver, heart, and skeletal muscle.

Our central hypothesis is that adipose tissue failure, rather than fat mass per se, drives the cardiometabolic complications of obesity. Understanding what limits expandability — and how to restore it — is a primary goal of the laboratory.

Organ Dysfunction Liver · Heart · Beta cells

Lipotoxicity & Fatty Liver Disease

When adipose tissue can no longer sequester excess lipid, free fatty acids and diacylglycerols accumulate in non-adipose organs. Our laboratory examines the molecular mechanisms by which lipid overload damages the liver (NAFLD/NASH), heart (diabetic cardiomyopathy), and pancreatic beta cells — and how these processes can be reversed therapeutically.

We use mouse genetic models, primary cell culture systems, and translational human tissue studies to dissect the lipotoxic cascade from initial lipid overload to fibrosis, cell death, and organ failure.

Thermogenesis Brown fat · Beige fat · UCP1

Brown Fat & Thermogenesis

Brown adipose tissue (BAT) and beige fat within white depots dissipate chemical energy as heat through uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1) and related futile cycles. We explore the transcriptional and post-translational regulation of thermogenesis, the contribution of skeletal muscle thermogenesis, and the potential of activating brown and beige fat as a therapeutic strategy for obesity and metabolic syndrome.

Recent work from our laboratory has identified UCP1-independent thermogenic mechanisms — including futile cycling of calcium and creatine — that substantially expand our understanding of adaptive heat production in adipose tissue.

Immunometabolism Macrophages · Inflammation · Insulin resistance

Macrophage Biology in Adipose Tissue

Adipose tissue macrophages (ATMs) are key regulators of local and systemic metabolic homeostasis. During obesity, ATMs shift from an anti-inflammatory, tissue-remodelling phenotype towards a pro-inflammatory state that drives fibrosis, cytokine release, and systemic insulin resistance. We study the signals governing ATM polarisation and how targeting macrophage biology could restore metabolic health.

Using single-cell genomics, flow cytometry, and in vivo mouse models, we are mapping the landscape of ATM heterogeneity and identifying the cellular and molecular checkpoints that control the transition from metabolically healthy to dysfunctional adipose tissue.
