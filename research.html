Our Research

Research Overview

The TVP Lab addresses the fundamental biology of energy metabolism and adipose tissue, with a particular focus on understanding why and how the body's capacity to safely store fat becomes overwhelmed — and what happens next at the cellular and systemic level.

Our work spans four interconnected research programmes, each tackling a distinct but related dimension of metabolic disease: white adipose tissue expandability, lipotoxicity, brown fat thermogenesis, and the immunometabolic function of macrophages within adipose depots.

Adipose Biology

White Adipose Tissue Expandability

White adipose tissue (WAT) acts as the primary buffer for surplus dietary energy. We investigate the molecular determinants of WAT expandability — the genes, signalling pathways, and epigenetic regulators that allow adipocytes to safely hypertrophy and hyperplasia — and the consequences when this capacity is exceeded, leading to ectopic lipid deposition in liver, heart, and skeletal muscle.

Organ Dysfunction

Lipotoxicity & Fatty Liver Disease

When adipose tissue can no longer sequester excess lipid, free fatty acids and diacylglycerols accumulate in non-adipose organs. Our laboratory examines the molecular mechanisms by which lipid overload damages the liver (NAFLD/NASH), heart (diabetic cardiomyopathy), and pancreatic beta cells — and how these processes can be reversed therapeutically.

Thermogenesis

Brown Fat & Thermogenesis

Brown adipose tissue (BAT) and beige fat within white depots dissipate chemical energy as heat through uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1) and related futile cycles. We explore the transcriptional and post-translational regulation of thermogenesis, the contribution of skeletal muscle thermogenesis, and the potential of activating brown and beige fat to treat obesity and metabolic syndrome.

Immunometabolism

Macrophage Biology

Adipose tissue macrophages (ATMs) are key regulators of local and systemic metabolic homeostasis. During obesity, ATMs shift from an anti-inflammatory, tissue-remodelling phenotype towards a pro-inflammatory state that drives fibrosis, cytokine release, and systemic insulin resistance. We study the signals governing ATM polarisation and how targeting macrophage biology could restore metabolic health.
